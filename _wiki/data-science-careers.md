## Definition and Scope

Data science careers run through [[data-scientist-role|data scientist roles]] and adjacent analytics, machine learning, research, and product roles. That path is a role-fit question rather than a title chase. Candidates need to know which version of data science they want. They also need to show matching work and ask whether the company has enough data maturity for the role to succeed.

The title can mean product analytics or applied [[machine learning]]. It can also mean experimentation, research, dashboards, or first-data-hire work. Given that ambiguity, candidates should look at responsibilities, team setup, and objectives before treating a data scientist title as a career step ([[podcast:data-science-job-red-flags-and-mismatched-roles|Data Science Jobs]]). When the responsibilities are mostly pipelines or reporting, the better comparison may be [[Data Engineering]] or the [[Data Analyst Role]].

For the interview path, use the [[Data Scientist Interview Roadmap]] and the [[Data Scientist Interview]] guide. For broader search mechanics, use [[Job Search]], [[CV Screening]], and [[Salary Negotiation]].

## Role Targeting Before Applications

Career advice converges on one order of work: choose a target role, find the missing skills, build evidence, and use applications to test fit. From the recruiter side, stronger CVs come from industry alignment, real projects, and concrete business problems, and tailored applications matter because candidates need to map their skills to the company's problem ([[podcast:get-data-scientist-job|Land Data Scientist Roles]]).

A similar frame organizes the search around goals and networking, then CV and strategy, and asks candidates to define the target role before collecting more courses or tools ([[podcast:job-search-strategy-in-tech-projects-skills-cv-networking|Tech Job Search Strategy]]). This belongs with [[Career Transitions in Data]]: without a target role, every portfolio project and CV bullet targets a different job.

There is also a company-side check covering role clarity, data team signals, and data maturity ([[podcast:data-science-job-red-flags-and-mismatched-roles|Data Science Jobs]]). A candidate should ask whether the team has data engineering support, analytics context, clear objectives, and realistic expectations for the career stage.

## Different Routes Into the Same Field

Candidates need practical evidence, but they start from different material. [[person:ksenialegostay|Ksenia Legostay]] moved gradually from project management into analytics and then machine learning: a skills gap assessment first, then planning, stakeholder communication, and KPI work as transferable strengths, and analysis work turned into a portfolio before moving deeper into machine learning ([[podcast:project-manager-to-data-scientist|From Project Manager to Data Scientist]]).

[[person:andradaolteanu|Andrada Olteanu]] took a more public-project route, using Kaggle notebooks and GitHub to turn analytics experience into data science evidence while keeping data validation, domain knowledge, and exploratory analysis as analyst strengths rather than background to discard ([[podcast:analytics-to-data-science-with-kaggle-portfolio|Career Transition from Analytics to Data Science]]).

From the hiring side, career changers are assessed on practical experience, portfolio projects, and online courses on a CV ([[podcast:hiring-data-scientists-and-analysts|Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts]]). The same distinction appears in [[podcast:job-search-strategy-in-tech-projects-skills-cv-networking|Tech Job Search Strategy]]: courses can close skill gaps, but projects and role-specific stories support the application.

## Skills as Role Evidence

Skills are evidence for a role, not a universal checklist. Programming, statistics, and domain expertise come first, with Git, testing, Docker, deployment, and clean code added because data science work has to leave the notebook when a team depends on it ([[podcast:project-manager-to-data-scientist|From Project Manager to Data Scientist]]).

The day-to-day role can be framed by its deliverables: trained models, pipelines, reports, and presentations, with stakeholder communication as a major challenge ([[podcast:how-to-break-into-data-science|Data Science Career Playbook]]). This puts communication beside modeling and Python rather than after the technical work.

For [[Data Science]], candidates still need SQL and statistics plus Python, modeling, and evaluation. ML-heavy roles add [[MLOps]], testing, deployment judgment, and the ability to explain production tradeoffs. Product data science roles lean harder on metrics, experimentation, stakeholder questions, and business framing.

## Portfolio Evidence

A data science portfolio should show how the candidate works, not only show notebooks. Portfolio strength ties to use-case alignment ([[podcast:get-data-scientist-job|Land Data Scientist Roles]]): each project should show the problem, the tools, the candidate's contribution, and the business change or decision the work supported.

The same point holds from the interview side: build a project to stand out, and use cold-start projects, synthetic data, and blogging for candidates without industry experience ([[podcast:data-science-interview-and-cv-guide|Data Science Interview Guide]]). [[Machine Learning Portfolio Projects]] expands that idea into project examples.

Public work can help, but the format depends on the target role. Kaggle notebooks and GitHub worked for an analytics-to-data-science transition ([[podcast:analytics-to-data-science-with-kaggle-portfolio|Career Transition from Analytics to Data Science]]), and relevant projects with real-world data are also recommended ([[podcast:how-to-break-into-data-science|Data Science Career Playbook]]). Product data science projects need business reasoning and metrics. ML-heavy projects need modeling, evaluation, and production judgment.

## CVs, Interviews, and Offers

Recruiters and hiring managers screen for clarity first, weighing profile screening, education signals, and CV clarity, and buzzword-heavy CVs make it harder to understand what the candidate did ([[podcast:hiring-data-scientists-and-analysts|Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts]]).

A similar screen treats the CV as a landing page for the role, emphasizes personal contribution and removing noise, and moves from business goals to evaluation metrics in case studies ([[podcast:data-science-interview-and-cv-guide|Data Science Interview Guide]]).

Use that advice with [[CV Screening]], behavioral stories, SQL, and ML fundamentals. Take-home tasks belong in the same preparation plan.

Candidates should also evaluate the company while the company evaluates them, weighing take-home burden, role clarity, and salary transparency ([[podcast:data-science-job-red-flags-and-mismatched-roles|Data Science Jobs]]), along with offer components and negotiation ([[podcast:data-science-interview-and-cv-guide|Data Science Interview Guide]]). When the conversation moves from interviews to offers, [[Salary Negotiation]] helps separate market evidence from wishful thinking.

## Career Progression and Adjacent Paths

Data science careers don't always move from junior data scientist to senior data scientist. Ksenia Legostay's path shows a project manager keeping planning and stakeholder strengths while adding statistics, programming, and machine learning. Andrada Olteanu's path shows an analyst keeping data validation and domain knowledge while making Python, notebooks, and public projects visible. Both examples belong with [[Career Transitions in Data]] because the transition depends on the evidence already available.

Adjacent roles can be better fits at different points. Candidates who like dashboards, stakeholder questions, and exploratory analysis may fit [[Data Analyst Careers]] before a modeling-heavy data scientist role. Pipeline, orchestration, and reliability work may fit [[Data Engineering]]. For model deployment and platform work, compare the target data scientist role with [[MLOps]] and machine learning engineering expectations.
