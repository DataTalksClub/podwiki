Academic researcher to data science is the transition from thesis or postdoc
work into industry data roles. It can also start from lab, simulation, or
research software work. The core move isn't abandoning research. It's
translating research evidence into the
language of [[data science]],
[[machine learning]],
[[data engineering]], or
[[applied research]].

The transition is a skills-translation problem plus a software-practice gap.
Evolutionary biology gave [[person:cjjenkins|CJ Jenkins]]
statistics, experiments, generalized linear models, and genomics data handling,
along with Bash, R, Python, and SQL. Her first industry role forced her to learn
APIs, infrastructure, Docker, and production collaboration
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

That places the topic inside
[[Academia]],
[[Career Transitions in Data]],
and [[Notebook to Production AI Systems]].

## Turning Research Work into Industry Evidence

Academic researchers usually don't need to discard their research history. The
research already contains useful data work; the missing industry stack has to be
named honestly and the story mapped to a specific role.

CJ rewrote a six-page academic CV into a skills-first resume, moving education
and publications out of the lead position and using recruiter feedback and
LinkedIn skill terms across about 14 iterations
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

The shared move is translation rather than reinvention. For CJ, translation
starts with genomics text files and Bash processing, then data cleaning,
statistics, and generalized linear models
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

Collider physics translates the same way: large event datasets and statistical
analysis become data-science signals, while version control and CI/CD become
research-software signals, and "multivariate analysis" reads as machine learning
([[podcast:from-large-hadron-collider-to-data-science-research-software-engineering|From the Large Hadron Collider to Data Science]]).

The second requirement is production readiness. CJ knew the theoretical side but
had to learn APIs, infrastructure, Docker, and Python, plus clean code, pair
programming, and code review after entering industry
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).
Research-to-production ML makes the same point: researchers keep experimental
rigor but need reproducibility, code review, deployment, and the engineering
habits that turn models into maintainable systems
([[podcast:research-to-production-ml-systems-roadmap|Research to Production ML Systems]]).

## Data Science, Data Engineering, or Applied ML

The role target depends on which part of the research work gives the clearest
industry signal. CJ's path fits the
[[data scientist role]] because
her research background maps to statistics, experiments, and credit-risk
modeling
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).
[[person:danielegbo|Daniel Egbo]] moves radio astronomy
toward applied ML and data engineering because MEERKAT work depends on source
detection, catalog cross-matching, and curated datasets, with later project work
adding cloud notebooks, Spark, and warehouse pipelines
([[podcast:from-radio-astronomy-to-machine-learning-and-data-engineering|From Radio Astronomy to ML and Data Engineering]]).
The boundary between these targets connects to
[[Data Engineer vs Data Scientist]]
and
[[Machine Learning Engineer vs Data Scientist]].

Some transitions point toward consulting or open-source proof instead of the
classic data-scientist funnel. [[person:orellgarten|Orell Garten]]
turns simulation research into problem discovery and MVP feedback, with custom
ETL and industrial data integration central to that path
([[podcast:from-academic-research-to-data-engineering-freelancing|From Academic Research to Data Engineering Freelancing]]).

A biology-to-ML path can lean on open-source computer vision and transformer
projects to replace a missing industry track record
([[podcast:from-biology-to-machine-learning-data-science-portfolio-open-source-computer-vision-transformers|From Biology to Machine Learning]]).
Those routes sit closer to
[[Data Engineering Portfolio Projects]]
and
[[Open Source Portfolio Evidence]].

Seniority also changes the evidence. Junior candidates need visible code,
learning agility, and a clear contribution story; CJ looks for people who can
absorb new information, take feedback, and admit when they were wrong
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).
At staff level, academic leadership, grants, collaborations, and fast stack
ramp-up have to become evidence for roadmap judgment, cross-functional
influence, ML design, and system design
([[podcast:from-academia-to-staff-ai-engineer-interviews-and-career-growth|From Academia to Staff AI Engineer]]).

## Role Targeting

The first practical question is which job the research background supports.
CJ's first industry target was statistical modeling in fintech, not a generic
"data" job. Her N26 interview accepted R for the case study because the team
wanted to see whether she knew the modeling concepts
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).
That version of the transition maps to
[[Data Science Careers]],
[[Data Scientist Role]], and
the [[data-scientist-role|Data Scientist Guide]].

Researchers whose work centers on pipelines, instruments, or large scientific
datasets may have a stronger path through data engineering or ML engineering.
Daniel's MEERKAT work includes cross-matching and uncertainty handling, then
later portfolio work with orchestration, object storage, Spark, and warehouse
pipelines
([[podcast:from-radio-astronomy-to-machine-learning-and-data-engineering|From Radio Astronomy to ML and Data Engineering]]).

Orell's simulation background leads toward industrial data integration,
custom ETL, and consulting delivery, with a stack that includes Docker and dbt
([[podcast:from-academic-research-to-data-engineering-freelancing|From Academic Research to Data Engineering Freelancing]]).
Those paths connect to the
[[Data Engineer Roadmap]]
and
[[Machine Learning Engineer Roadmap]].

## Translating Research into Hiring Evidence

The strongest translation names the industry equivalent of the academic task.
Rather than asking fintech interviewers to value de novo transcriptomes for
their own sake, CJ reframes that work as large-file handling, Bash processing,
messy data cleaning, statistical modeling, and domain translation
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

Collider "multivariate analysis" and research tooling translate the same way
into machine learning and statistical analysis, with version control and CI/CD
supporting the story
([[podcast:from-large-hadron-collider-to-data-science-research-software-engineering|From the Large Hadron Collider to Data Science]]).

The resume should also name the product or decision context. Recruiters look for
clear project-to-tech-stack links, use cases, and business impact; academic
candidates can struggle when they describe research as knowledge discovery
instead of product mindset or productionized work
([[podcast:get-data-scientist-job|How to Get a Data Scientist Job]]).

Tailor the application to the role, treat the CV as a landing page, and prepare
project stories that show personal contribution
([[podcast:data-science-interview-and-cv-guide|Data Science Interview Guide]]).
That makes this topic adjacent to
[[CV Screening]],
[[Job Search]], and the
[[data-scientist-interview|Data Scientist Interview Guide]].

## Production and Collaboration Skills

Researchers need enough software practice to explain how analysis becomes work
someone else can run. CJ names APIs, infrastructure, Docker, and Python as early
gaps, and notes that current junior candidates need stronger coding than she had
when she started, including clean code, pair programming, and code review
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

Full-lifecycle ML systems extend that into tools such as PyTorch and Docker,
with cloud infrastructure, web frameworks, reproducibility, code review, and
deployment in the same production path
([[podcast:research-to-production-ml-systems-roadmap|Research to Production ML Systems]]).
Those skills connect to
[[Software Engineering]] and
[[Production]]. They also connect to
[[Reproducibility]],
[[MLOps]], and
[[Machine Learning System Design]].

The collaboration shift is just as important as the stack. The move includes
simplifying explanations for non-academic colleagues, learning Slack norms, and
leaving academic competitiveness behind; industry collaboration often means
sitting next to someone and sharing one keyboard, and being willing to look
uninformed while learning
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).
That links the transition to
[[Communication]],
[[Team Building]], and
[[Leadership]].

## Portfolio and Interview Proof

For most non-research industry roles, publications are weak evidence; they matter
more when the role is explicitly research-oriented. A portfolio is useful when it
shows effort, but many pet projects use clean datasets that don't resemble messy
production data
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

For an academic candidate, stronger proof often comes from exposing the messy
data work inside the research: large files, shell processing, data cleaning, and
modeling choices, plus the explanation that makes the work legible outside the
field
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

Good project shapes include:

- A reproducible science-data pipeline
  ([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).
- A catalog or cross-matching project with uncertainty handling
  ([[podcast:from-radio-astronomy-to-machine-learning-and-data-engineering|From Radio Astronomy to ML and Data Engineering]]).
- An IoT prototype or proof of concept that exposes data integration and
  feedback loops
  ([[podcast:from-academic-research-to-data-engineering-freelancing|From Academic Research to Data Engineering Freelancing]]).
- An open-source contribution with clear domain context
  ([[podcast:from-biology-to-machine-learning-data-science-portfolio-open-source-computer-vision-transformers|From Biology to Machine Learning]]).
These examples connect to
[[Machine Learning Portfolio Projects]],
[[Data Engineering Portfolio Projects]],
[[Portfolio Projects]], and
the [[Open Source Contributor Roadmap]].

Interview proof should match the role and level. CJ's first technical interview
included a case study in R and a Python-code walkthrough where she had to reason
through unfamiliar syntax honestly
([[podcast:postdoc-to-data-science-lead-career-transition|Postdoc to Data Science Lead]]).

The physics-to-computer-vision version covers Kaggle projects and
collaborations, data collection and labeling, and deployment with Docker that
makes the work reviewable. Implementation practice covers Python and SQL, and
interview preparation covers algorithms, system design, LeetCode, and mock
interviews
([[podcast:from-physics-to-computer-vision-career-transition|From Physics to Computer Vision]]).

At staff level, the same path covers coding practice, ML design, and system
design, plus mock interviews, referrals, mentorship, and production onboarding
([[podcast:from-academia-to-staff-ai-engineer-interviews-and-career-growth|From Academia to Staff AI Engineer]]).
That evidence belongs with
[[Data Scientist Interview Roadmap]]
and [[Staff AI Engineer]].
