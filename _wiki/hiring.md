## Definition and Scope

Hiring covers how the podcast archive describes finding, evaluating, and growing
data and AI talent. The archive includes recruiters, heads of data, data science
managers, technical leads, and candidates. Together they show hiring as a
systems problem: define the role, understand the market, write a usable job
description, source candidates, screen evidence, run interviews, evaluate fit,
negotiate fairly, and onboard people into a team that can use their skills.

This page focuses on employer-side lessons. Candidate tactics belong in
[Job Search]({{ '/wiki/job-search/' | relative_url }}), though the two pages
share evidence because hiring process and candidate preparation mirror each
other.

## Recurring Archive Themes

### Start from the problem, not a tool list

Recruiter and manager episodes warn against job descriptions built from long
technology wish lists. Stronger hiring starts with the business problem, team
context, responsibilities, level, and success criteria. This helps candidates
self-select and helps recruiters challenge unrealistic requirements.

### Role titles are unstable

The archive repeatedly shows title ambiguity. "Data scientist" can mean product
analytics, ML engineering, experimentation, research, BI, or dashboard work.
"Data engineer" can overlap with BI engineering, platform work, DevOps, or
analytics engineering. Hiring teams need to define the actual work before they
screen CVs or design interviews.

### Hiring level changes the evidence

Junior, senior, expert, manager, and staff candidates should not be assessed the
same way. Junior hiring values learning velocity, foundations, practical
projects, and mentoring capacity. Senior hiring adds ownership, abstraction,
stakeholder work, and judgment. Expert hiring values deep technical or domain
strength. Manager hiring tests team building, communication, prioritization,
feedback, and strategy.

### Interviews should match the job

Technical interviews appear across the archive, but guests warn against
syntax-heavy tests or take-home work that does not resemble the role. Good
interviews test the kind of reasoning the person will use: SQL and Python for
data engineering, product metrics for product data science, scenario design for
ML systems, stakeholder tradeoffs for managers, and practical domain judgment
for specialized roles.

### Hiring continues after the offer

Team-growth episodes treat onboarding, feedback, mentorship, regular check-ins,
psychological safety, and career frameworks as part of hiring quality. A team
that hires juniors without support or managers without clear expectations will
misread performance problems as hiring mistakes.

## Hiring Patterns

### Recruiter-led funnel

Recruiters work with hiring managers on role definition, market feedback,
sourcing, screening, candidate preparation, salary conversations, and offer
communication. The archive's recruiter episodes show this as a feedback-heavy
process, not only CV forwarding.

### Manager-led assessment

Managers decide what the role needs and how to evaluate it. Episodes on data
science management, B2B SaaS data teams, and NLP teams emphasize practical
assessment: project walkthroughs, case studies, scenario questions, code or SQL
tests where relevant, and discussion of tradeoffs.

### Build-vs-buy team growth

Teams decide whether to hire a senior person, hire an expert, hire a manager,
or grow juniors. The archive preserves the tradeoff: juniors create long-term
organizational strength but need support; experts solve complex technical
problems but may not want people management; managers create leverage through
coordination and development.

### Inclusive and transparent hiring

Guests discuss inclusive job language, salary bands, avoiding discouraging
requirements, and evaluating career breaks. The practical pattern is to remove
signals that do not predict job performance and to make expectations clear
enough for a wider candidate pool to self-select.
