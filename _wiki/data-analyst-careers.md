## Definition and Scope

Data analyst careers cover entry and growth paths for people who use data to
explain what happened, quantify problems, define metrics, build dashboards, and
support decisions. In the podcast archive, analyst careers sit close to product
and business context. They also rely on SQL, BI tools, experiments, and
stakeholder communication.

For role responsibilities, read [Data Analyst Role]({{ '/wiki/data-analyst-role/' | relative_url }}).
For the boundary with modeled and tested BI-ready data layers, read
[Data Analyst vs Analytics Engineer]({{ '/wiki/data-analyst-vs-analytics-engineer/' | relative_url }}).

## Recurring Archive Themes

**Analyst work is decision work.** The archive doesn't frame analysts as
dashboard producers only. Analysts help product managers and leaders decide
whether a problem deserves work. They also show which users are affected,
whether an experiment moved the right metric, and where a number can be trusted.

**SQL and business context are the core signals.** Analyst career evidence often
starts with SQL, BI tools, metrics, and domain knowledge. Python, R, and light
modeling help. Guests still place the strongest weight on knowing where data
lives, which definitions matter, and how to explain results to stakeholders.

**Internal moves are realistic entry points.** Project managers, business
analysts, operations people, marketers, and product specialists can build
analyst evidence by analyzing data they already use at work. This path makes
prior domain knowledge visible and can produce better proof than a standalone
course certificate.

**Titles vary across companies.** "Data analyst", "product analyst", "business
analyst", and "analytics engineer" can describe overlapping work. Recruiting
advice for analysts is similar to data scientist recruiting, but screening
keywords and responsibilities differ. Candidates should read the job description
for tasks, team context, and expected SQL, BI, statistics, and product depth.

**Analyst growth can branch.** The archive shows several growth directions:
senior product analyst, analytics engineer, data scientist, data product work,
experimentation, and data engineering-adjacent roles. The useful career question
isn't which title sounds more advanced, but which work the person wants to own.

## Episode Evidence

These episodes give the strongest analyst-career evidence.

- [Data Team Roles Explained](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  7:51-10:39, analysts understand company data, build dashboards, define KPIs,
  quantify product problems, and evaluate whether a model-backed feature helped.
  At 18:17-19:08, analyst writing is aimed at management and decision makers.
- [From Analytics to Production ML](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  18:39-24:23, analyst work includes ad hoc SQL, reports, recommendations,
  product-team support, and business-metric modeling. At 24:23-28:42, analysts'
  data knowledge helps teams find trustworthy data.
- [From Analytics to Production ML](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  31:19-33:30, analysts help explain experiment uplift, segment differences,
  and root causes when online results diverge from offline model quality.
- [Project Manager to Data Scientist](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  32:43-39:09, a project manager-to-data path starts by analyzing work data,
  using BI tools, learning Python, joining communities, and learning from Kaggle
  notebooks.
- [Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html):
  At 54:09-59:30, analyst hiring is discussed through ambiguous titles, similar
  recruiting steps, different screening keywords, and the limits of assuming
  that a title reveals the work.
- [Data-Led Growth, Event Tracking, and Reverse ETL](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html):
  At 46:13-51:40, analysts work with data engineers, analytics engineers, and
  product operations around tracking plans, dashboards, and activation
  workflows.
