People negotiate salary in data and AI career conversations to decide whether
the money, level, risk, and work match. In DataTalks.Club podcast discussions,
the topic appears in recruiter screens and offer conversations. It also appears
in salary ranges in job descriptions and freelance pricing. It sits next to
[Job Search]({{ '/wiki/job-search/' | relative_url }}),
[Hiring]({{ '/wiki/hiring/' | relative_url }}),
[Job Descriptions]({{ '/wiki/job-descriptions/' | relative_url }}), and
[Freelance]({{ '/wiki/freelance/' | relative_url }}).

The episodes treat salary negotiation as a market check tied to the role, not
as a contest over an arbitrary number. Candidates bring evidence of fit, level,
and value. Competing options matter too.

Employers bring salary bands, leveling, budget, and hiring urgency. Freelancers
use another calculation because they price client risk and unpaid selling time.
They also price legal setup, taxes, and gaps between projects.

## Early Pay Conversations

Compensation comes up before the final offer. In
[Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast/hiring-data-scientists-and-analysts.html),
[Alicja Notowska](https://datatalks.club/people/alicjanotowska.html) says around
36:25 that a recruiter screen usually covers notice period and availability.
It also covers current interview activity and salary expectations. Recruiters
need that context early enough to avoid pulling a candidate through interviews
when the gap is too large.

[Luke Whipps](https://datatalks.club/people/lukewhipps.html) gives the
candidate-friendly boundary in
[Land Data Scientist Roles](https://datatalks.club/podcast/get-data-scientist-job.html).
At 52:22, he says asking about salary early isn't a red flag when candidates
want to avoid wasted calls. He also treats low salary expectations as a sign
that a candidate may be underpaid, not as proof of weak ability. That makes
salary transparency part of [Job Search]({{ '/wiki/job-search/' | relative_url }})
and [Hiring]({{ '/wiki/hiring/' | relative_url }}), not only a closing tactic.

[Oleg Novikov](https://datatalks.club/people/olegnovikov.html) maps the
candidate side in
[Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast/data-science-interview-and-cv-guide.html).
At 13:24, he describes the usual path from recruiter screen to offer. At 42:02,
he asks candidates to compare the full offer package with market baselines. At
50:17, he says candidates with low current salary should avoid making the old
number the anchor. The company has already interviewed them and decided they
have value for the new role.

## Bands, Anchors, and Fairness

Alicja separates current salary from expected compensation around 40:33 in
[Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast/hiring-data-scientists-and-analysts.html).
Candidates don't need to disclose current salary. They can first ask how the
company handles levels and bands. If the company has clear bands, a senior
candidate shouldn't fall into a lower band because they named a low number. If
the company leaves pay mostly to negotiation, naming a low range can become a
costly anchor.

The recruiter and candidate perspectives differ most on timing. Alicja says at
45:05 that recruiters may ask what a high number is based on. Market data,
Glassdoor-style reports, and other offers make the number easier to understand.
A random high number can end the conversation if the candidate treats it as a
fixed demand.

Oleg's 50:17 advice in
[Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast/data-science-interview-and-cv-guide.html)
pushes the other way. Once the company has shown interest, the candidate can
ask what the company can do when the offer feels low. They don't have to defend
an old salary.

[Tereza Iofciu](https://datatalks.club/people/terezaiofciu.html) puts salary
ranges inside job-description quality in
[Data Science Jobs](https://datatalks.club/podcast/data-science-job-red-flags-and-mismatched-roles.html).
At 37:08, she treats ranges as a fairness and trust signal. Missing ranges can
show internal pay problems, regional inconsistency, or weak hiring discipline.
Candidates should read salary range, role scope, and team maturity together
when they evaluate [Job Descriptions]({{ '/wiki/job-descriptions/' | relative_url }}).

## Market Evidence

Candidates can make salary expectations stronger by explaining the market
evidence behind them. Alicja's 40:33 advice in
[Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast/hiring-data-scientists-and-analysts.html)
starts with the company's compensation structure. She recommends asking how
leveling and salary bands work before naming a range. In the same episode, she
says candidates can update expectations after research or another offer. At
1:01:49, she asks them to keep recruiters informed when other offers or timing
changes affect a decision.

Market research needs to match the real job, not just the title. Oleg notes at
15:29 in
[Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast/data-science-interview-and-cv-guide.html)
that "data scientist" can mean product analytics, SQL-heavy experimentation, or
production machine learning. Salary research should therefore sit beside
[Data Scientist Role]({{ '/wiki/data-scientist-role/' | relative_url }}),
[Data Engineer Role]({{ '/wiki/data-engineer-role/' | relative_url }}), and
[Data Science Careers]({{ '/wiki/data-science-careers/' | relative_url }}).

[Sarah Mestiri](https://datatalks.club/people/sarahmestiri.html) makes the same
point from job-search strategy. At 20:01 in
[Tech Job Search Strategy](https://datatalks.club/podcast/job-search-strategy-in-tech-projects-skills-cv-networking.html),
she tells candidates choosing between data engineering, MLOps, and ML
engineering to study the job market. Target salary and target environment
become filters alongside skills, interests, and demand.

## Negotiating Power Before the Offer

Candidates can strengthen their position before anyone discusses numbers. In
[Land Data Scientist Roles](https://datatalks.club/podcast/get-data-scientist-job.html),
Luke ties recruiter confidence to clear CVs and industry alignment at 16:15,
19:50, and 25:04. He also names project evidence and business impact. Those
signals make a candidate easier to place and easier for a hiring manager to
justify.

Oleg gives the same job-search mechanics in
[Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast/data-science-interview-and-cv-guide.html).
At 18:28, he says the CV should work like a landing page. At 25:51, he asks
candidates to show personal contribution and remove noise. Stronger
[CV Screening]({{ '/wiki/cv-screening/' | relative_url }}) evidence helps the
later salary discussion because the candidate has already shown fit for the
specific role.

Competing offers create the strongest position. Oleg says at 42:02 that
candidates should compare offer components and market baselines. At 50:17, he
still gives options to candidates without another offer. They can compare the
offer with staying or declining, or ask how the company can bridge the gap. The
conversation then centers the candidate's decision rather than their old
salary.

Communication also affects long-term reputation during offer talks. Alicja
warns at 1:01:49 in
[Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast/hiring-data-scientists-and-analysts.html)
that accepting an offer and reopening it later because another offer arrived
damages trust. Candidates can keep options open, but they shouldn't surprise a
recruiter after making a commitment. That belongs with
[Career Growth]({{ '/wiki/career-growth/' | relative_url }}) because the
relationship can outlast one hiring round.

## Portfolio Evidence

Portfolio proof gives candidates a reason to argue for the higher side of a
range. Oleg says at 46:17 in
[Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast/data-science-interview-and-cv-guide.html)
that candidates without industry experience can create evidence through
projects, writeups, and company-specific work. A useful project shows problem
definition, modeling or analysis, and evaluation. It also shows communication
and next steps.

Sarah makes portfolio work part of the job search. At 26:28 in
[Tech Job Search Strategy](https://datatalks.club/podcast/job-search-strategy-in-tech-projects-skills-cv-networking.html),
she treats projects as skill validation beyond course completion. At 59:46,
she adds that explaining what you learned helps later in interviews. Public
explanation can support salary negotiation because it makes skill and judgment
visible before the offer.

Luke's recruiter advice gives the same evidence. At 19:50 and 25:04 in
[Land Data Scientist Roles](https://datatalks.club/podcast/get-data-scientist-job.html),
he wants portfolios to connect tools and projects to real use cases, including
business impact. Portfolio evidence belongs with
[Machine Learning Portfolio Projects]({{ '/wiki/machine-learning-portfolio-projects/' | relative_url }}),
[Data Engineering Portfolio Projects]({{ '/wiki/data-engineering-portfolio-projects/' | relative_url }}),
and
[Open Source Portfolio Evidence]({{ '/wiki/open-source-portfolio-evidence/' | relative_url }}).

Portfolio evidence doesn't replace market data, but it makes a market argument
credible. A candidate who asks for the top of a range should be able to show
why the role, level, and demonstrated work support that number.

## Freelance Pricing

Freelance and consulting pricing isn't salary negotiation with a different
unit. The person is pricing skill and availability. They also price client
risk and unpaid selling time. Taxes, legal setup, and gaps between projects
matter too. That's why [Freelance]({{ '/wiki/freelance/' | relative_url }}) is
adjacent to salary negotiation rather than a synonym.

[Adrian Brudaru](https://datatalks.club/people/adrianbrudaru.html) explains the
risk calculation in
[Freelance Data Engineering Playbook](https://datatalks.club/podcast/freelance-data-engineering-pricing-and-clients.html).
At 7:06, he frames freelance income through occupancy rate. At 18:12, he
discusses hourly rates and negotiation. At 53:02, he warns that charging the
equivalent of a salary can fail because the freelancer still has occupancy risk
and business overhead.

[Dimitri Visnadi](https://datatalks.club/people/dimitrivisnadi.html) gives a
channel-based pricing model in
[Becoming a Data Freelancer](https://datatalks.club/podcast/becoming-data-freelancer.html).
At 25:24, he separates platform work and recruiter channels from direct work.
He also covers other client-acquisition paths. At 27:30 and 28:50, he
recommends benchmarking against platform profiles, directories, and recruiter
ranges. The client's expected budget matters too.

At 54:11, he adds runway planning before leaving employment.

Employment negotiation often rests on competing offers, level fit, and the
company's band. Freelance pricing rests more on positioning, proof, network,
and client urgency. Dimitri says at 55:01 that people often misprice freelance
work when they assume credentials alone justify high rates. Adrian says at
55:30 in
[Freelance Data Engineering Playbook](https://datatalks.club/podcast/freelance-data-engineering-pricing-and-clients.html)
that good clients expect proactivity, ownership, and outcomes.
