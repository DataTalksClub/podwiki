## Definition and Scope

Salary negotiation covers how candidates and recruiters discuss compensation
during data and AI hiring. In the podcast archive, salary appears in recruiter
screens, offer evaluation, job description transparency, market research, and
candidate trust. The recurring message is pragmatic: know the market, understand
the company's compensation structure, avoid unnecessary current-salary
disclosure, and keep the conversation tied to role value and level.

This page focuses on employment offers. Freelance pricing and consulting rates
belong in [Freelance]({{ '/wiki/freelance/' | relative_url }}).

## Recurring Archive Themes

### Current salary is not the anchor

Recruiter and interview-prep episodes agree that candidates do not need to
share current salary. The better frame is the expected compensation for the new
role, based on level, skills, market data, location, competing processes, and
the value the company says it sees.

### Ask how the structure works

Before naming a number, candidates should ask whether the company uses levels
and salary bands. If bands exist, a low initial number should not pull the offer
below the band. If compensation depends heavily on negotiation, candidates need
to be more careful about anchoring too low.

### Use ranges and keep expectations updateable

The archive supports giving a researched range rather than a single rigid
number. It is reasonable to say that expectations may change after more market
research, more interview information, or competing offers.

### Research protects both sides

Glassdoor, public salary data, recruiter feedback, peer conversations, and
offer comparisons help candidates avoid random numbers. Recruiters may ask what
a number is based on, especially when the number sits far outside the expected
range.

### Leverage comes from alternatives

Competing offers create the strongest negotiation power. Without another offer,
candidates still have alternatives: staying in the current role, asking for a
sign-on bonus, clarifying lost bonus or promotion value, or declining when the
move does not make sense.

### Transparency builds trust

Luke Whipps argues that recruiters should share company name, salary, job spec,
and other decision information early enough for candidates to avoid wasted
calls. Tereza Iofciu treats missing salary transparency in descriptions as a
signal candidates should investigate.
