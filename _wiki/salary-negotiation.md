## Definition and Scope

Salary negotiation covers how candidates and recruiters discuss compensation
during data and AI hiring. In the podcast archive, salary appears in recruiter
screens and offer evaluation. It also appears in job description transparency,
market research, and candidate trust.

The recurring message is pragmatic. Candidates should know the market,
understand the company's compensation structure, avoid unnecessary
current-salary disclosure, and tie the conversation to role value and level.
For consulting rates, read [Freelance]({{ '/wiki/freelance/' | relative_url }}).


## Recurring Archive Themes

Don't anchor on current salary. Recruiter and interview-prep episodes agree that
candidates don't need to share current salary. Candidates should frame expected
compensation around the new role's level and skills. Market data, location,
competing searches, and company value also matter.

Ask how the structure works. Before naming a number, candidates should ask
whether the company uses levels and salary bands. If bands exist, a low initial
number shouldn't pull the offer outside that band. If compensation depends
heavily on negotiation, candidates should avoid anchoring too low.

Use ranges and keep expectations updateable. The archive supports giving a
researched range rather than one rigid number. It's reasonable to say that
expectations may change after more market research, more interview information,
or competing offers.

Glassdoor, public salary data, and recruiter feedback help candidates avoid
random numbers. Peer conversations and offer comparisons help too. Recruiters
may ask what a number is based on when it sits far outside the expected range.

Competing offers create the strongest negotiation position. Without another
offer, candidates can still compare against staying in the current role. They
can also ask for a sign-on bonus, clarify lost bonus or promotion value, or
decline when the move doesn't make sense.

Luke Whipps argues that recruiters should share the company name, salary, job
spec, and other decision information early enough for candidates to avoid wasted
calls. Tereza Iofciu treats missing salary transparency in descriptions as a
signal candidates should investigate.

## Episode Evidence

These episodes give the strongest evidence on salary conversations.

- [Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html):
  At 40:33-45:05, Alicja Notowska says candidates don't need to disclose current
  salary. They should ask how salary structure and leveling work. They can give
  a range when bands exist.
- [Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html):
  At 45:05-47:36, she recommends grounding high expectations in market research,
  other offers, or clear rationale. Large unexplained gaps can stop the hiring
  discussion, but recruiters often try to find alternatives when the gap is
  small.
- [Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  42:02-43:56, Oleg Zhero advises candidates to understand offer components,
  compare market baselines, and negotiate. He also recommends using competing
  offers when possible and asking about sign-on bonuses.
- [Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  50:17-52:30, he advises candidates not to reveal low current salary and to
  frame the discussion around skills and company value. Candidates can also
  compare the offer with staying or declining.
- [Land Data Scientist Roles](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  52:22-54:12, Luke Whipps says asking about salary early isn't a red flag when
  candidates need to avoid wasted time. At 1:03:56-1:06:01, he discusses low and
  high salary signals from a recruiter perspective.
- [Data Science Jobs](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At 30:20-35:52,
  Tereza Iofciu discusses salary transparency in job descriptions and why
  missing salary information can expose deeper compensation and fairness
  problems.
