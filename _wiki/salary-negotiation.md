## Definition and Scope

Salary negotiation covers how candidates and recruiters discuss compensation
during data and AI hiring. In the podcast archive, salary appears in recruiter
screens, offer evaluation, job description transparency, market research, and
candidate trust.

The recurring message is pragmatic. Candidates should know the market,
understand the company's compensation structure, avoid unnecessary
current-salary disclosure, and tie the conversation to role value and level.
For consulting rates, read [Freelance]({{ '/wiki/freelance/' | relative_url }}).

## Recurring Archive Themes

**Do not anchor on current salary.** Recruiter and interview-prep episodes agree
that candidates don't need to share current salary. Candidates should frame
expected compensation around the new role's level, skills, market data,
location, competing processes, and company value.

**Ask how the structure works.** Before naming a number, candidates should ask
whether the company uses levels and salary bands. If bands exist, a low initial
number shouldn't pull the offer below the band. If compensation depends heavily
on negotiation, candidates should avoid anchoring too low.

**Use ranges and keep expectations updateable.** The archive supports giving a
researched range rather than one rigid number. It's reasonable to say that
expectations may change after more market research, more interview information,
or competing offers.

**Ground numbers in research.** Glassdoor, public salary data, recruiter
feedback, peer conversations, and offer comparisons help candidates avoid random
numbers. Recruiters may ask what a number is based on when it sits far outside
the expected range.

**Use alternatives as negotiation power.** Competing offers create the strongest
negotiation position. Without another offer, candidates can still compare
against staying in the current role, asking for a sign-on bonus, clarifying lost
bonus or promotion value, or declining when the move doesn't make sense.

**Look for transparency.** Luke Whipps argues that recruiters should share the
company name, salary, job spec, and other decision information early enough for
candidates to avoid wasted calls. Tereza Iofciu treats missing salary
transparency in descriptions as a signal candidates should investigate.

## Episode Evidence

These episodes give the strongest evidence on salary conversations.

- [Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html):
  At 40:33-45:05, Alicja Notowska says candidates don't need to disclose current
  salary, should ask how salary structure and leveling work, and can give a range
  when bands exist.
- [Hiring Data Scientists and Analysts](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html):
  At 45:05-47:36, she recommends grounding high expectations in market research,
  other offers, or clear rationale. Large unexplained gaps can stop the process,
  but recruiters often try to find alternatives when the gap is small.
- [Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  42:02-43:56, Oleg Zhero advises candidates to understand offer components,
  compare market baselines, negotiate, use competing offers when possible, and
  ask about sign-on bonuses.
- [Data Science Interview Guide](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  50:17-52:30, he advises candidates not to reveal low current salary and to
  frame the discussion around skills, company value, and the alternative of
  staying or declining.
- [Land Data Scientist Roles](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At
  52:22-54:12, Luke Whipps says asking about salary early is not a red flag when
  candidates need to avoid wasted time. At 1:03:56-1:06:01, he discusses low and
  high salary signals from a recruiter perspective.
- [Data Science Jobs](https://datatalks.club/podcast.html): At 30:20-35:52,
  Tereza Iofciu discusses salary transparency in job descriptions and why
  missing salary information can expose deeper compensation and fairness
  problems.
