## Definition and Scope

Career growth covers what happens after someone has entered data, analytics, ML,
AI engineering, or data leadership work. The archive frames growth as a shift
from proving baseline skill to increasing scope: owning bigger problems,
debugging harder systems, influencing stakeholders, mentoring others, making
work visible, and choosing between individual-contributor and management paths.

This page focuses on archive patterns rather than promotion advice in the
abstract. It covers ML engineering growth, staff-level AI work, data leadership,
technical writing, public speaking, community visibility, mentoring, and the
skills hiring managers use to distinguish junior, senior, expert, and manager
roles.


## Recurring Archive Themes

### Growth means scope, not only sharper tools

The archive repeatedly moves seniority away from tool checklists. Senior people
debug ambiguous problems, define tradeoffs, mentor teammates, communicate with
stakeholders, and choose where technical effort changes the product. They still
need technical depth, but the growth signal is how they use that depth.

### T-shaped people adapt across waves

Episodes on ML engineering, AI engineering, and applied LLM research favor a
T-shaped shape: stable fundamentals such as SQL, Git, shell, debugging, Python,
statistics, and system design, plus selective depth in the current problem area.
This helps explain why guests can move from web to games to ML platforms, from
academia to staff AI, or from software engineering to VP-level ML leadership.

### Visibility is work, not vanity

Technical writing, public speaking, open-source contribution, mentorship, and
community participation all recur as ways to make work inspectable. The archive
does not treat visibility as a substitute for competence. It treats visibility
as a way to teach, clarify thinking, get feedback, and make future collaborators
or hiring managers understand what someone can do.

### Communication becomes a senior technical skill

Senior growth episodes emphasize explaining tradeoffs, connecting projects to
business priorities, writing design documents, giving feedback, pitching
experiments, and presenting to executives. In these episodes, communication is
not soft decoration around technical work. It is how technical work gets
adopted.

### Management is a separate transition

Several guests distinguish growth as an individual contributor from growth as a
manager. Management adds feedback, hiring, team development, prioritization,
stakeholder alignment, and psychological safety. Some guests describe movement
between IC and manager tracks rather than a permanent one-way promotion ladder.

## Growth Patterns

### Junior to mid-level

The archive's junior-growth pattern is to improve craft quality and ownership:
write maintainable code, document decisions, ask better questions, ship
projects, and learn enough surrounding context to avoid waiting on others for
every decision.

### Mid-level to senior

Senior growth adds abstraction, delegation, broader stakeholder exposure, and
stronger judgment about what not to build. Guests describe senior people as
better at turning vague business or product goals into tractable data work.

### Senior to staff or expert

Staff and expert growth centers on influence beyond one project. Staff AI
engineering episodes cover strategy, roadmapping, cross-functional influence,
architecture, code review load, and mentoring. Expert tracks preserve deep
technical and domain skill when a company needs high-quality modeling or
specialized judgment.

### Individual contributor to manager

Manager growth is a role transition. Guests describe learning feedback,
one-on-ones, hiring, prioritization, team development, and stakeholder
translation. Former hands-on expertise helps, but it does not replace the
manager's job of creating conditions for other people to do good work.

### Practitioner to public technical voice

Writing, talks, tutorials, and DevRel work create a public record of judgment.
The archive links this pattern to teaching, portfolio strength, open-source
adoption, and career options that sit between engineering, product, education,
and community.
