## Definition

The podcast archive treats data scientist interview preparation as a staged
project. Candidates need to understand the target role, show relevant project
evidence, pass recruiter screens, handle technical rounds, reason through
business cases, tell concise behavioral stories, and evaluate offers.

This roadmap isn't a list of interview questions. It converts recruiter, coach,
and hiring-manager episodes into a preparation sequence. The recurring lesson
is that candidates should prepare for the actual role, not an abstract "data
scientist" title.

## Stage 1: Pick the Target Role Type

Start by reading job descriptions for responsibility, not title. A product data
scientist, ML-heavy data scientist, research scientist, first data hire, and
analytics-heavy data scientist get tested differently.

Write a one-page target profile. Include domain, expected tools, likely
interview rounds, common data, product or business context, and skills you can
defend. The archive's hiring episodes repeatedly warn that the data scientist
title is ambiguous.

## Stage 2: Make the CV and Portfolio Easy to Screen

Prepare a CV that shows relevant projects, business or product impact, tech
stack, and personal contribution. Remove noise. Recruiters and hiring managers
need to see quickly why your experience matches this role.

Build or polish one portfolio project that fits the target market. A good
project connects the problem, data, method, metric, tradeoff, and result. It
isn't enough to list a model and a notebook.

## Stage 3: Prepare Recruiter and Intro Screens

Practice a short career narrative, a role-fit explanation, salary and location
expectations, and a few STAR stories. Research the company, interviewer, and
product. Use the first call to clarify the interview stages and technical
depth.

The recruiter-led episodes describe this as alignment work. If you know the
rounds and expectations, you can study the right depth instead of preparing for
every possible interview.

## Stage 4: Practice Technical Fundamentals

Cover SQL, Python, statistics, ML fundamentals, evaluation metrics, data
cleaning, feature thinking, leakage, validation, and coding. Tie each technical
claim to a project you can explain.

If you say you know a model, be ready to describe when it fails, what
assumptions it makes, and how you evaluated it. For ML system design or case
rounds, start with the business goal, user, decision, data, metric, constraint,
baseline, and evaluation path before naming an algorithm.

## Stage 5: Prepare Case, Behavioral, and Offer Rounds

Case interviews test how you structure ambiguity. Behavioral interviews test
ownership, communication, collaboration, conflict, failure, and impact. Prepare
stories in a grid so the same project can answer different prompts without
sounding scripted.

Before offers, prepare market research, priorities, constraints, and
negotiation boundaries. Hiring episodes treat offer discussions as part of the
process, not as an afterthought.

## Practice Sequence

Prepare these materials in order.

1. Target role map: list role types you want and role types you won't pursue.
2. CV rewrite: top-half summary, relevant projects, measurable impact, tools,
   and personal contribution.
3. Portfolio case study: problem, data, method, metric, result, limitation, and
   next step.
4. Technical drill set: SQL windows and joins, Python data manipulation,
   probability, statistics, model evaluation, and one favorite model.
5. Case-study template: goal, users, data, assumptions, metric, baseline, model
   or analysis, risks, and validation.
6. Behavioral story grid: conflict, failure, leadership, ambiguity,
   collaboration, stakeholder management, and impact.
7. Process tracker: company, role type, recruiter notes, rounds, feedback,
   follow-up, and offer details.

## Role Milestones

Application readiness means a recruiter can understand your target role,
project relevance, and impact within the first scan of your CV or profile.

Screen readiness means you can explain your story, target role, salary and
logistics, and the project evidence behind your claims.

Technical readiness means you can solve SQL and Python basics, explain ML
fundamentals, reason through metrics and validation, and defend one or two
projects in depth.

Final-round readiness means you can structure ambiguous business cases, tell
concise behavioral stories, ask role-clarifying questions, and compare offers
against your goals.

## Interview Boundary

Stop studying and start interviewing when you can already do this work.

- Explain the specific data scientist role you want.
- Walk through one relevant project without reading notes.
- Solve common SQL and Python exercises under time pressure.
- Explain model evaluation, leakage, validation, and tradeoffs.
- Answer behavioral prompts with short, impact-first stories.
- Ask recruiters what each round will test.

Don't wait until you can answer every ML question. The archive recommends
targeted preparation and feedback. Use early interviews to learn the market,
then update your CV, project, and practice plan.
