## Definition

Career transitions in data are the paths people take from adjacent fields into
data science, data engineering, analytics, ML engineering, AI engineering,
freelancing, or leadership. The archive treats transitions as evidence-building
problems: candidates need proof of skill, relevant stories, and role fit.

## What the Archive Says

The archive contains many transition paths: project managers to data scientists,
analysts to data scientists, software engineers to ML engineers, academics to
industry, DevOps engineers to data engineering, career returners to AI
engineering, and employees to freelancers.

The recurring advice is not “take one course.” It is to translate prior
experience into the target role, build artifacts, learn enough engineering to
ship, and make the story legible to hiring managers or clients.

## Key Patterns

### Transferable skills matter, but must be translated

Software engineers bring system-building. Analysts bring metric and business
context. Academics bring research depth but often need deployment, Docker, APIs,
and interview practice. Career changers need to map prior work into recruiter
and manager language.

### Portfolios work when they are specific

The strongest portfolio advice favors domain-specific, documented, reproducible,
deployed, or open-source projects. Generic tutorial clones are weaker signals.

### Public learning creates accountability and visibility

Writing, public leaderboards, open source, talks, and community participation
appear repeatedly as ways to learn, get feedback, and build trust.

### Freelancing requires business proof

Freelance and consulting episodes emphasize market validation, referrals,
network-first acquisition, specialization, value-based pricing, and financial
runway.

## Tradeoffs and Contradictions

The archive praises public work and portfolios, but hiring episodes warn that
visibility alone is not enough. The best signal is relevant proof matched to the
company’s actual problem.
