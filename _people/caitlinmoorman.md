## Background in Data and Operations

Caitlin Moorman joined DataTalks.Club as VP of Data and Business Operations at Trove Recommerce. She discussed [Last-Mile Data Delivery for the Modern Data Stack: Build Data Products to Boost Adoption]({{ '/podcasts/last-mile-data-delivery-and-data-product-adoption-modern-data-stack/' | relative_url }}). In that conversation, she connected her work across crowdfunding and self-publishing through recommerce to data work that changes decisions.

In archive terms, Caitlin connects [data teams]({{ '/wiki/data-teams/' | relative_url }}) with [business intelligence]({{ '/wiki/business-intelligence/' | relative_url }}), [analytics engineering]({{ '/wiki/analytics-engineering/' | relative_url }}), and [leadership]({{ '/wiki/leadership/' | relative_url }}). She kept the technical stack in view, but focused most of the discussion on the human, operational, and managerial work that turns data into action.

## Last-Mile Data Delivery

Caitlin framed the "last mile" as the stage where data reaches the decision point. The [modern data stack]({{ '/wiki/modern-data-stack/' | relative_url }}) made ingestion, transformation, warehouses, and basic dashboards easier. She argued that those improvements don't guarantee impact. Teams can still feel that data is unavailable, analysts can feel undervalued, and business teams can continue making decisions outside the data.

Data teams can apply that distinction to [data products]({{ '/wiki/data-products/' | relative_url }}). Getting clean data into a warehouse and a dashboard solves the scale problem. Getting a team to change behavior requires understanding the specific stakeholders, meetings, incentives, and decision routines where the data should appear. If data doesn't arrive at that moment, the team hasn't finished the delivery work.

## Adoption and Stakeholder Workflows

Caitlin treated [data product adoption]({{ '/wiki/data-product-adoption/' | relative_url }}) as product work. When people don't use a dashboard, model, or report, the team needs to understand why. Stakeholders may not know it exists, trust it, know how to use it, or believe it answers their real question. She compared that diagnosis to user research.

With that product mindset, Caitlin described stakeholder work differently. She didn't start with an A/B testing dashboard and then decide what data to join. She recommended starting with the decision a product manager needs to make. The manager may need to ship, wait, or understand business impact.

From there, the team can choose the right data sources, transformations, and interface. She also recommended sitting in the meetings where decisions happen. The team can sketch low-fidelity dashboard or deck ideas, then ask whether the proposed view would help the stakeholder decide.

## BI Delivery, Metrics, and Trust

Caitlin's view of [business intelligence]({{ '/wiki/business-intelligence/' | relative_url }}) wasn't limited to dashboards. She emphasized discoverability, interpretability, trust, and training because each one lowers the cost of using data. If stakeholders can't find the data, understand it, or trust it, they fall back to gut judgment or personal spreadsheets.

She also tied [metrics]({{ '/wiki/metrics/' | relative_url }}) to incentives. People use data when better decisions matter to their goals. When teams have clear targets around conversion, acquisition cost, or operational efficiency, they have a reason to ask which action matters most. For hard-to-measure work, Caitlin recommended practical proxies. Teams can use time studies, surveys, or narrow before-and-after comparisons instead of waiting for perfect measurement.

## Leadership and Influence

Caitlin described analytics [leadership]({{ '/wiki/leadership/' | relative_url }}) as narrowing the first use case enough to create a visible win. She suggested finding a stakeholder who already wants to use more data, solving a meaningful but tractable problem, and turning that person into an advocate. Financials and large cost centers can reveal high-impact opportunities, but she warned against starting with the most resistant stakeholder just because their area is large.

For resistance, Caitlin recommended selling upside, not cuts. A data leader should ask what's blocked today, improve a painful manual workflow when possible, and build rapport before pushing for broader change.

For exploratory data work, she separated linear projects from circular projects. Teams can plan some projects step by step, but other projects require investigation before the team knows what's possible. In circular cases, leaders preserve trust by naming the uncertainty early. They should report progress in small pieces and offer alternatives when the data can't support the original path.
